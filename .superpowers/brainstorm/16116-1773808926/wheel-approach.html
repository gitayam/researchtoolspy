Behaviour Change Wheel — Visual Approach

How should we render Michie's concentric wheel in your app?

A

Interactive SVG Wheel (Faithful Reproduction)

A true concentric ring diagram matching Michie's original BCW. Three rings: COM-B hub (6 segments), intervention functions (9 segments), policy categories (7 segments). Click segments to drill in, highlight deficit areas, animate connections between layers.

Pros: Instantly recognizable to practitioners. Shows relationships spatially. High visual impact.
Cons: Complex SVG/Canvas work. Mobile responsiveness is tricky for circular layouts.
B

Hybrid: Wheel Overview + Card Drill-Down

The wheel serves as a visual navigation hub at the top. Clicking a segment scrolls to detailed cards below. Deficits are color-coded on the wheel (red/amber/green). The wheel is read-only but interactive for navigation — actual data entry stays in familiar card forms.

Pros: Best of both worlds — visual overview + usable forms. Practitioners see the wheel, analysts work in cards.
Cons: Two UI paradigms to maintain. Wheel is "decorative-functional" not primary input.
C

Full Wheel-Driven UX (Wheel IS the Interface)

The wheel is the primary interaction surface. Click COM-B segments to assess deficits inline. Selected deficits light up intervention segments automatically. Select interventions to illuminate policy segments. Radial tooltips and slide-out panels for detail. The entire workflow flows outward from hub to rim.

Pros: Most faithful to Michie's methodology flow (COM-B → Interventions → Policies). Deeply immersive.
Cons: Highest complexity. Accessibility challenges. May feel unfamiliar for data entry.
My recommendation: Option B (Hybrid)

It gives practitioners the iconic wheel they expect while keeping the data-entry UX accessible and mobile-friendly. The wheel becomes a powerful summary visualization that also serves as navigation — matching how Michie herself uses the wheel (as a diagnostic/summary tool, not a data-entry form).
